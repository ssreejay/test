Introduction

This session explores the nature and role of values in evaluation. Most simply stated, evaluation involves the use of systematically gathered information in order to make a judgement about the value (merit, worth, or significance) of something. One of the most basic conflicts in the evaluation literature with regard to valuing relates to whether there can be any sort of objective basis for valuing. A second and related controversy focuses on whether it can ever be justified for evaluators to align themselves with one stakeholder position against another. We will examine the implications of these two issues for evaluation practice.

Can there be an objective basis for values?

Scepticism towards values has dominated science since at least the 17th century. Francis Bacon (1561-1626) was the first to attempt a description of ‘the scientific method’. He promoted a demarcation between science and non-science and argued that the distinguishing feature of the scientific method was that it was based on presuppositionless observation. In particular he argued that scientists must free themselves from four sorts of idols related, roughly, to culture, personal predispositions, ill-fitting pre-existing concepts (constructs) and the constraints of particular philosophical systems. To Bacon science involved “cleansing of the mind of all its presuppositions and prejudices, and reading the book of nature with fresh eyes” (Blaikie, 1993, p. 134).

The philosopher, David Hume (1711–1776) popularised the view that ‘values’ (normative statements about merit and worth) cannot be derived from ‘facts’ (descriptive statements of what is). This is sometimes referred to as the ‘is-ought’ problem or Hume’s Guillotine. According to this perspective, a distinction must be maintained between facts and values, with the latter being systematically ‘walled off’ from having any influence.

The ‘fact-value’ distinction or ‘values-free’ doctrine has had a profound influence on social research and evaluation. Although evaluators generally accept that facts and values are intertwined, and cannot be neatly separated, evaluators still struggle to understand the nature and role of valuing. When engaging with values, evaluators often look to theory or systematic observation, or some combination of both.

Drawing evaluation criteria from a theoretically developed ethical perspective is referred to as prescriptive valuing. Drawing criteria from values expressed by stakeholders is called descriptive valuing.

Prescriptive valuing

Prescriptive theories of value have received considerable emphasis in philosophy and economics but little in evaluation. Typically evaluators have advocated a descriptive approach in which goals or criteria are clarified with stakeholders. Even in evaluation, however, particular ethical perspectives can be seen in choices made at every stage of the evaluation—it is just that they are usually unacknowledged and uncritiqued. For example, even in an evaluation that focuses on the stated goals of a program as determined by managers, utilitarian principles underlie the choice of an outcome focus and most probably the choice of allegiance to those who are perceived to have the power to make decisions.

Defintions:

  • Utilitarianism is an ethical perspective first formalised by Jeremy Bentham but most fully enunciated by John Stuart Mill in which the ethical decision is considered to be that which results in the greatest overall sum of happiness, regardless of how it is distributed.
  • Deontological ethical systems include means as well as ends within the ethical framework. They emphasise due process, duties and rights as well as expected outcomes.
  • Consequentialism refers to systems where actions are judged according to their expected or actual consequences. It includes utilitarianism but also other systems such as those that seek to promote the well-being of the most disadvantaged as a first concern (e.g. Rawls “Justice as fairness” has had a major influence in evaluation.

The definitions above summarise a number of commonly utilised ethical perspectives. In evaluation, it is rare to find cases or methods that exclusively operationalise one perspective. Cost-benefit analysis, a form of economic evaluation in which all benefits are transformed into money values, is probably the most pure implementation of utilitarianism.

Empirical bases for prescriptive theories: Scriven

Shadish et al. (1991) argue that Scriven’s focus on needs analysis and “functional analysis” as the source of criteria for evaluation is essentially prescriptive in that it seeks to privilege certain value claims above others (pp. 96-99). Scriven is whole-heartedly consequentialist in his orientation but favours a “justice as fairness” perspective over pure utilitarianism as the basis for determining ‘need’. Scriven’s strong rejection of ‘goals’ as a source of evaluation criteria is markedly anti-descriptive

Also, in insisting that evaluators attempt a “synthesis” resulting in a single statement about the merit of a program, Scriven requires evaluators to utilise criteria for weighting and adjudicating between value claims—criteria that would normally be developed from some prescriptive framework. Scriven’s approach differs from other prescriptive approaches in that it seeks to derive criteria empirically and inductively rather than through deductive reason, as is the common approach of philosophers and economists. He is searching for a ‘science of valuing’, and empirical ethics that goes beyond the mere collation of stakeholder preferences. The methodology of this science is unclear but it has something to do with describing the nature of human needs and the essential function of objects/programs in relation to those needs. The program is judged by the extent to which it meets those functions without deleterious side effects.

Descriptive valuing

While prescriptive ethical systems influence evaluators in many ways of which they may not be aware, the explicit processes for development of evaluation criteria are usually descriptive. That is, they involve procedures that determine what particular stakeholder groups think should occur and should be the basis for judging the program. This is probably an activity with which social scientists feel more at home than with philosophical debate.

There are numerous procedures that have been identified to tap the opinions of individuals and groups. Trochim’s (YEAR) concept mapping technique is one example of a systematic and robust process for identifying stakeholder values. By far the most common approach to descriptive valuing is, however, to simply accept the stated goals of the program as the criteria by which the program should be judged. Unfortunately the dangers of such an approach are immense as goals are usually written for purposes other than elucidating what the program can really be expected to achieve (such as obtaining funding). Wholey and Patton (YEAR) among others have emphasised the need to carefully review goals and formulate them in realistic terms both in relation to the programs theory of action and the resources and knowledge available.

What is the place for descriptive and prescriptive approaches to valuing?

As we have seen, value claims, as with knowledge claims are formulated through a combination of empirically derived information and deductive reason. Any set of values adopted as the basis for judgement in evaluation will involve both descriptive and prescriptive elements, although, the prescriptive elements are frequently not conscious let alone explicit.

Shadish et al. (1991) criticise Scriven for his emphasis on prescriptive theory and argue that they give priority to descriptive theory with prescriptive theory in predominantly a safeguard role providing a “source of discipline about criteria and about ethical implications of certain choices” (p 98). Their concerns with prescriptive theories are:

  • there is no agreed ethical framework but rather many competing frameworks all with strong arguments in their favour
  • prescriptive theories are not operational in most-decision-making and are therefore likely to be of little interest and use to policy makers
  • the contesting of values is the role of political processes, a role that should not be usurped by the evaluator
  • the ‘public interest’ perspective that Scriven advocates as the basis for needs identification is ill defined.)

Scriven’s opinion in relation to these concerns is reflected in his view that the purely descriptive approach to valuing reflects ‘the lackey view of evaluation research’. Scriven would argue that in aligning themselves with ‘decision-makers’ evaluators have made a series of de facto value and ethical judgements and risk co-option in the perpetuation of injustice. House and Howe (1999) express similar concerns. This leads us into the issue of competing stakeholder perspectives and the decision by evaluators to align themselves with one or another perspective.

The issue of alignment not only affects the source of evaluation criteria but also how and to whom the evaluation reports. Not surprisingly the authors listed under the consumerist heading in the above table tend to advocate the widest possible distribution of evaluation findings where the others are more likely to favour private reports and briefings.

Scriven, in contrast to his unwillingness to draw criteria directly from stakeholders, advocates distributing a draft report to the widest possible range of stakeholders to obtain the greatest possible range of perspectives. He suggests then including their critiques, interpretations and comments in the final report.

Summing-up

There are a number of questions about valuing and evaluation criteria that we might ask about any evaluation perspective that we examine:

  • What is the role of explicit and implicit ethical frameworks within this perspective?
  • Does this perspective assume a particular theory of
    • justice?
    • duty?
    • rights?
    • utility?
    • quality?
  • In what ways does this perspective seek to empirically develop or validate its criteria?
  • What is the role of descriptive valuing? Whose values are likely to be sought?
  • Does this approach advocate or imply a primary concern with the needs of one stakeholder group?

Scriven’s (1993) Hard-Won Lessons in Program Evaluation This was the basis for an entire edition of New Directions for Program Evaluation (No.58). Incidentally, this series later revised its title to New Directions for Evaluation at the urging of Scriven.

Hard-Won Lessons is organised around thirty-one ‘theses’ (more like aphorisms) that are grouped into categories. I have provided Chapter 1 only. It is the lengthiest and most substantive of the seven chapters, and is perhaps the finest exposition of Scriven’s dogged perspective on the nature and fundamentals of evaluation.

The volume may have partly been inspired as a counterpoint to Lee J. Cronbach and associates' (1980) earlier and equally provocative ninety-five theses on evaluation, that were articulated at length in the book Toward Reform of Program Evaluation. Scriven clearly disagrees with Cronbach’s applied social science approach to evaluation (see especially, pp.40-41). Written in characteristic Scrivenesque style, Hard-Won Lessons contains gems such as: “Side effects are often the main point” (p. ref), “An evaluation without a recommendation is like a fish without a bicycle” (p. ref), and “One can only attain fourth-generation evaluation by counting backward” (p. ref).
